How happy do you feel right now? That’s the question that Somerville, Massachusetts is asking its residents. The city started conducting its bi-annual happiness survey in 2011 to measure the overall happiness and well-being of the city's residents, and to identify factors that contribute to or detract from their happiness. The results are used to inform city planning and policy decisions, with the aim to improve the quality of life for residents, and to make the city a better place to live and work.

Since its start, the city has collected over 10,000 responses from Somerville residents. Across 10 years of data, residents have reported an average happiness rating of 7.5 out of ten. The most recent year of data, 2021, had the lowest average happiness rating yet, but year-to-year differences are marginal.

21% of the variance in current happiness is explained by satisfaction with Somerville, according to city analysts. While factors like general life satisfaction explain a greater portion of happiness variance, the city government focuses its efforts on the 21% of variance that policy decisions can affect. The majority of a resident’s satisfaction with Somerville is explained by satisfaction with their specific neighborhood, but survey data has allowed analysts to identify even more detailed determinants of a resident’s happiness rating.

“The maximum effect is the maximum amount that ‘satisfaction with Somerville’ can be improved by moving from a 1 to the maximum of the scale (5 or 10, depending on the question),” said SomerStat Analyst Logan Hughes. “‘Maximum effect’ is measured by multiplying the scale (either 5 or 10 points, depending on the question) by the estimate in the regression.”

Transportation is the single most significant factor when it comes to a resident’s satisfaction with Somerville. The question “How convenient is it for you to get where you want to go?” has a maximum effect rating of 1.9.

Somerville recently saw the single largest expansion of the MBTA subway system in decades, placing a subway stop within a 12-minute walk of nearly the entire city, according to Somerville Mayor Katjana Ballantyne.

Next, “the availability of information about city services” and “how satisfied were you with your ability to access city services?” have the second and third highest maximum effect ratings of 1.3 and 1.2 respectively.

“How satisfied are you with the beauty or physical setting of your neighborhood?” comes fourth with a maximum effect rating of 1.2. In this vein, Somerville has set out an ambitious plan to create 105 new acres of open space by 2040. Since 2010, the city has created 15 acres of new open space with four more acres in the pipeline.

“In addition to adding acreage, the City has focused on renovating existing parks and schoolyards that are in the poorest condition,” according to the city’s SomerVision2040 report. “Twenty parks have been renovated since 2010 and most of the schoolyards either have been recently renovated or have planning grants to put this process in motion.”

“How safe do you feel walking in your neighborhood at night?” ranks as the fifth most influential factor, with a maximum satisfaction rating of 1.1.

Working to improve safety, the city created a “Public Safety for All” initiative in recent years that involves community members in proposing new policies around “internal police reforms, the investment of City funds toward equitable and accessible public safety projects, crime prevention, and more.”

Still, the maximum effect rating might not be the most intuitive rating for many people to understand (myself included). If you count yourself in that group, you can also compare average ratings of factors like general life satisfaction, satisfaction with Somerville, and satisfaction with your neighborhood compared against average ratings of happiness, from 1 to 10. Factors that are positively correlated with happiness will increase as happiness ratings increase.

Keep in mind when looking at the data that the "City Services Information," "Social Events Availability," and "Housing Costs" lines are based on survey questions that use a five point scale. In comparison to questions that use a 10 point scale, those lines will look less significant than they actually are. The availability of information about city services, for instance, has the second highest maximum effect score of any of the variables included in SomerStat's analysis. Data depicted here is calculated exclusively from the most recent year of the survey, 2021.
